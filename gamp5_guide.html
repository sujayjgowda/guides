01 — Introduction

What is GAMP 5?

GAMP 5 (Good Automated Manufacturing Practice, 5th Edition) is an industry standard issued by ISPE that provides practical guidance for validating computerized systems used in GxP-regulated environments such as pharmaceutical, biotech, and medical device industries.

Origin

Why GAMP Was Created

Regulatory bodies such as the FDA, EMA, and WHO require that all computerized systems impacting product quality, patient safety, or data integrity be validated. GAMP 5 emerged as the industry's consensus framework to operationalize this requirement in a practical, risk-based way — replacing the outdated one-size-fits-all approach to validation.

When to Use

Any time a computerized system is used in a GxP process — from LIMS and MES systems to ERP and laboratory equipment software.

Purpose

Core Objectives

GAMP 5 aims to ensure that computerized systems are fit for intended use, appropriately documented, and verifiably compliant. It promotes a scalable, science-based approach where the depth of validation effort is proportional to the complexity and risk of the system being validated.

How it Works

By categorizing software, assessing risk, applying life cycle controls, and generating a documented audit trail — proving to regulators that systems are validated and maintained.

"GAMP 5 is not about validating software; it is about validating that the system is fit for its intended use in a regulated environment — with evidence, proportionate to risk."

— ISPE GAMP 5 Guiding Principle
02 — Regulatory Context

Software Validation Standards

GAMP 5 aligns with and operationalizes a set of global regulatory standards and guidelines that define legal requirements for computerized system validation in life sciences.

FDA

21 CFR Part 11

The U.S. FDA's regulation governing electronic records and electronic signatures. It sets criteria under which electronic records and signatures are considered trustworthy and equivalent to paper records. GAMP 5 directly addresses Part 11 compliance through audit trails, access controls, and data integrity practices.

Applies When

Systems create, modify, maintain, archive, or transmit electronic records in FDA-regulated activities.

EU

EU Annex 11

The EU GMP Annex covering computerized systems used in regulated pharmaceutical manufacturing and quality systems. It requires validation, data integrity, audit trails, electronic signatures, and backup/recovery procedures. GAMP 5 serves as the primary guidance for achieving Annex 11 compliance.

Applies When

Operating in EU-regulated markets or manufacturing products exported to EU markets.

WHO

WHO Technical Report Series

WHO's guidance documents on computerized systems and data integrity are harmonized with GAMP principles and apply to manufacturers supplying global markets, including prequalification for UN procurement.

Applies When

Seeking WHO prequalification or supplying medicines for UN/WHO-funded programs.

ICH

ICH Q10 — Pharmaceutical Quality System

This guidance requires that pharmaceutical quality systems be supported by reliable data systems. GAMP 5's computerized system validation framework is the practical means of ensuring ICH Q10 compliance for electronic systems used in quality management.

Applies When

Establishing quality management systems with electronic controls and documentation.

03 — Software Classification

GAMP 5 Software Categories 1–5

GAMP 5 classifies software into five categories based on novelty, complexity, and the degree of supplier versus user configuration. This drives proportionate validation effort — more complex and custom software requires more rigorous validation.

1

Infrastructure Software

Commercial software that provides the platform on which other systems run. It is not directly configured for specific business functions. The supplier is responsible for this software's integrity, and it is used "as is." Validation focuses on confirming correct installation and suitability for the environment.

Examples: Operating Systems (Windows Server, Linux), Database engines (Oracle, MS SQL), Virtual machine platforms (VMware), Network services
When to Use

Validate through qualification of the IT environment, not through functional testing of the software itself. Obtain supplier documentation and confirm version suitability.

Low Risk
2

Non-Configured Software (COTS)

Commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) software that is used without any configuration or customization. The software operates exactly as delivered by the vendor. It is designed for a wide audience and not tailored to GxP use — examples include basic office tools used in ancillary processes.

Examples: Microsoft Word (for SOP editing), Adobe Acrobat (for document control), Standard calculators, basic reporting tools
When to Use

Demonstrate fitness for intended use through supplier audit, documentation review, and limited functional testing. No customization testing needed.

Low Risk
3

Configured Software

COTS software that is configured to meet specific user and business requirements. The underlying software engine is maintained by the supplier, but the user defines workflows, parameters, master data, and processes. This is the most common category in the pharmaceutical industry.

Examples: SAP ERP, Oracle EBS, Veeva Vault QMS, MasterControl, TrackWise, LIMS (LabWare, STARLIMS), Clinical Trial Management Systems (CTMS)
When to Use

Full CSV process required: URS, supplier assessment, risk assessment, IQ/OQ/PQ protocols, configuration testing, and training records. Focus testing on configured business workflows.

How to Validate

Test each configured process flow against requirements. Test boundary conditions. Verify audit trails, access controls, and data integrity controls function as configured.

Medium Risk
4

Configured Products with Custom Extensions

Software where the base product is configured AND custom code or modules are added on top of the standard platform. The custom extensions introduce additional risk because they are not covered by supplier testing. Both the configuration and the custom code must be validated.

Examples: SAP with custom ABAP modules, Salesforce with custom Apex code, Veeva with custom workflows, custom plugins to standard QMS platforms
When to Use

All Cat 3 validation activities PLUS source code review, developer testing documentation, and change control for the custom elements. Requires involvement of the development team in validation.

How to Validate

Apply a software development life cycle (SDLC) to the custom code. Conduct design review, unit testing, integration testing, and regression testing when custom elements are changed.

High Risk
5

Custom / Bespoke Software

Software that is developed entirely from scratch to meet specific GxP needs. There is no pre-existing supplier base; the full development and validation burden rests with the user organization. This carries the highest risk and requires the most comprehensive validation effort, including a complete SDLC.

Examples: In-house developed manufacturing execution systems, custom laboratory automation software, bespoke patient data management systems, proprietary regulatory submission tools
When to Use

Required when no commercial solution can meet GxP requirements. Justify the decision. Apply full SDLC: requirements → design → code → test → maintain.

How to Validate

Produce full specification suite (URS, FS, DS, module specs), execute all IQ/OQ/PQ phases, maintain source code under version control, and document all testing with traceability to requirements.

04 — Why GAMP?

Special Benefits of GAMP 5

GAMP 5 delivers tangible operational and regulatory benefits. Understanding these benefits helps organizations justify the investment in a structured validation programme and communicate its value to leadership.

⚖️

Regulatory Confidence

Provides a documented, defensible audit trail that satisfies FDA, EMA, and WHO inspectors — reducing the risk of 483 observations or warning letters.

🎯

Proportionate Effort

Risk-based approach ensures validation resources are invested where they matter most — eliminating wasted effort on low-risk systems.

🔒

Data Integrity

Ensures data is ALCOA+ compliant: Attributable, Legible, Contemporaneous, Original, Accurate — safeguarding product quality and patient safety.

🔄

Change Control

Structured change management prevents unvalidated changes from introducing risk into GxP systems — maintaining the validated state over the system's life.

📊

Business Continuity

Validated systems with proper backup, recovery, and contingency procedures reduce business disruption from system failures.

🤝

Supplier Leverage

Structured supplier assessments and quality agreements shift appropriate responsibility to software vendors — reducing in-house testing burden.

📉

Reduced Recalls & Deviations

Validated systems produce reliable data and controlled processes — directly reducing the frequency of quality failures, deviations, and product recalls.

🌍

Global Harmonization

GAMP 5 aligns with FDA 21 CFR Part 11, EU Annex 11, PIC/S, WHO, and ICH — one framework satisfying multiple regulatory jurisdictions.

05 — Foundational Theory

Key Concepts of GAMP 5

GAMP 5 is built on a set of interlocking foundational concepts. Understanding these is essential before applying any specific GAMP tool or process — they explain the "why" behind every validation activity.

Core

Risk-Based Approach

The central pillar of GAMP 5. Risk assessment determines what validation activities are needed and at what level of depth. Systems with high potential impact on patient safety or product quality receive the most rigorous validation. Low-risk systems receive proportionately less.

When Applied

At every stage — from initial system assessment through retirement. Every decision about testing scope, documentation depth, and change control is driven by risk.

How to Implement

Use formal FMEA or risk ranking tools. Document risk assessment reports. Link risks to test protocols. Review residual risks after mitigation.

Core

Scalable Life Cycle

GAMP 5 does not prescribe a fixed set of documents or tests. Instead, it defines a life cycle framework that each organization scales up or down based on software category, system complexity, and risk profile. This makes it applicable to a simple COTS tool or a complex bespoke system.

When Applied

During planning — to decide which phases, documents, and testing strategies to apply to a given project.

How to Implement

Prepare a Validation Plan that defines the scope, deliverables, and rationale for the chosen approach. Get it approved before starting.

Core

Intended Use

Validation is always tied to the system's intended use in a specific context. The same software might be used differently in different roles — each use case may require separate validation consideration. GAMP 5 insists that what is being validated is the use, not just the software.

When Applied

When writing URS. When defining test scope. When evaluating supplier documentation for relevance to your specific use case.

How to Implement

State the intended use explicitly in the Validation Plan and URS. Map every test case back to a specific use requirement.

Core

Supplier Involvement

GAMP 5 recognizes that much of the quality assurance work for software is performed by the supplier during development. Rather than re-doing this work, the user organization should assess, leverage, and give appropriate credit for supplier testing — focusing their own effort on configuration-specific and use-case-specific testing.

When Applied

Before purchasing a system. During qualification. When assessing whether to reuse supplier test evidence.

How to Implement

Conduct Supplier Audits or send questionnaires. Review design specifications, test protocols, and test summaries. Document which supplier evidence is accepted and why.

Phase 1–2

Concept & Planning

Define the business need, assign a project team, perform an initial risk assessment, categorize the system (GAMP cat 1–5), identify regulatory requirements, and produce a Validation Plan. The Validation Plan is the master governing document for everything that follows.

Key Deliverable

Validation Plan (VP) — approved by QA before project proceeds.

Phase 3–4

Specification & Build

Produce the User Requirements Specification (URS), then trace those requirements through Functional and Design Specifications. For configured/custom systems, configuration is performed against the approved specifications, and configuration records are maintained.

Key Deliverable

URS, FS/DS, Configuration Records — all subject to version control and formal review.

Phase 5

Testing & Verification

Execute IQ (Installation Qualification), OQ (Operational Qualification), and PQ (Performance Qualification) protocols. Each test must be pre-approved, executed with actual results, and any deviations formally resolved before proceeding. Generate Test Summary Reports.

Key Deliverable

IQ/OQ/PQ Protocols, executed Test Scripts, Deviation Reports, Test Summary Reports.

Phase 6–8

Release, Operate & Retire

Issue a Validation Summary Report (VSR) and obtain formal QA approval before go-live. Thereafter, maintain the validated state through change control, periodic reviews, security management, backup/recovery testing, and incident management. At retirement, archive all records per regulatory retention requirements.

Key Deliverable

VSR, Change Requests, Periodic Review Reports, Retirement Plan.

08 — Documentation

Validation Kit

The Validation Kit is the complete set of documents generated during the CSV process. Together, these documents constitute the evidence that the system is validated, and they form the audit-ready package presented to regulators during inspections.

📝

Validation Plan (VP)

Master governance document defining scope, approach, deliverables, roles, and schedule. Approved by QA before work begins.

📌

User Requirements Specification (URS)

Defines what the system must do from the user's perspective. Basis for all test script creation. Written in business language.

🏗️

Functional Spec (FS) / Design Spec (DS)

How the system will be designed to meet the URS. FS = functional behavior; DS = technical architecture. Required for Cat 4 & 5.

⚠️

Risk Assessment Report

Documents identified risks, their likelihood/impact, assigned mitigations, and residual risk. Drives testing depth and priorities.

🔍

Supplier Assessment Report

Results of supplier audit or questionnaire. Identifies which supplier documents can be leveraged and accepted as evidence.

🔧

IQ Protocol & Report

Pre-approved test procedure verifying correct installation of hardware, software, and network. Executed and signed off by IT and Validation.

⚙️

OQ Protocol & Report

Tests that the system functions according to its specifications across all configured features. Most detailed testing phase.

✅

PQ Protocol & Report

Verifies the system performs under real operational conditions — process simulations, volume testing, end-to-end workflow testing.

⚡

Deviation / Discrepancy Log

Tracks all unexpected test results. Each deviation must be formally investigated, resolved, and reviewed by QA before proceeding.

📊

Traceability Matrix (RTM)

Maps each URS requirement to its test scripts and test results — proving that every requirement was tested and passed.

📃

Validation Summary Report (VSR)

Final document summarizing all validation activities, results, deviations resolved, and conclusion that the system is validated and fit for use.

🎓

Training Records

Proof that all users and administrators have been trained on the validated system before going live. Required by 21 CFR Part 11 and Annex 11.

09 — End-User Responsibility

Customer Validations

In GAMP 5, the regulated user (the "customer" of the software, whether buying from a vendor or deploying internally) retains ultimate regulatory responsibility for validating the system in their specific environment and use context — regardless of what the supplier has tested.

Principle

Non-Transferable Responsibility

No matter how thoroughly a supplier has validated their software, the regulated organization must verify that it works correctly in their specific infrastructure, with their specific configuration, for their specific business processes. Supplier testing covers the product; customer validation covers the use.

When Applies

Always — even for SaaS and cloud-hosted systems. Customer must validate their data, their workflows, and their configuration of the hosted tool.

SaaS Era

Cloud & SaaS Validation

GAMP 5's 2022 supplement addresses cloud and SaaS systems specifically. The supplier manages the infrastructure (IQ equivalent), but the customer must still validate their configured workflows (OQ) and ensure their business processes perform correctly (PQ). Quality agreements with the cloud provider are essential.

When Applies

For any GxP SaaS tool — Veeva Vault, Medidata Rave, Microsoft 365 in regulated environments, cloud LIMS, etc.

Documentation

What the Customer Must Produce

Even when heavily leveraging supplier documentation, the customer organization must produce their own: Validation Plan, URS (their requirements in their words), Risk Assessment (their risk context), test protocols (their use cases), executed test records, and a Validation Summary Report signed by their QA.

How to Do It

Start with supplier docs and gap assess. Write your URS independent of supplier documentation. Your URS captures your needs — not a copy of the supplier's manual.

Ongoing

Maintaining Validated State

After go-live, every change — upgrades, patches, configuration changes, process changes, infrastructure changes — must go through change control. The customer must assess impact, test as needed, and update their validation documentation. This is perpetual, not a one-time task.

How to Do It

Implement a formal Change Request process. Assess every change for validation impact. Use a categorized approach: minor changes = documentation update; major changes = re-testing.

10 — Evaluation Types

3 Types of Assessments in GAMP 5

GAMP 5 defines three distinct types of assessments that are performed at different points in the system life cycle. Each serves a different purpose and produces different outputs that feed into the overall validation strategy.

1. System Assessment

GxP Impact & Category Determination

Performed at the beginning of any new or existing system evaluation. Determines whether the system is GxP-relevant, what GAMP category it falls into, and what regulatory requirements apply.

  • Is the system GxP-relevant?
  • What GAMP category (1–5)?
  • What regulations apply?
  • What is the data criticality?
  • What validation approach is required?
When Used

Before any new system implementation and periodically as part of system inventory reviews.

2. Risk Assessment

Hazard Analysis & Mitigation Planning

Identifies and evaluates risks associated with the system's functions and use in a GxP context. Determines which functions are critical and drives the depth and scope of validation and testing activities.

  • Identify critical functions (CFs)
  • Score risks (Likelihood × Impact)
  • Define mitigation controls
  • Determine residual risk acceptability
  • Link risks to test requirements
When Used

After URS is drafted; before designing test protocols. Repeated when significant changes are made.

3. Supplier Assessment

Vendor Qualification & Evidence Review

Evaluates the software supplier's quality management system, development practices, and available testing documentation. Determines what supplier evidence can be leveraged, reducing the user organization's internal testing effort.

  • Does supplier have a QMS (ISO 9001)?
  • Is there documented SDLC evidence?
  • Are release notes and test reports available?
  • Is the supplier auditable?
  • What can be accepted as-is?
When Used

Before purchasing software and periodically thereafter (typically every 2–3 years or on major releases).

11 — Applied Principles

5 Key Concepts Applied Through GAMP 5

These five concepts cut across every phase of the GAMP 5 life cycle. They are not separate tools but rather lenses through which every validation decision must be viewed — ensuring that validation remains practical, defensible, and genuinely fit for purpose.

Concept 01 — Proportionality

Scaled Effort Based on Risk

Validation effort must be proportionate to the risk the system poses to product quality, patient safety, and data integrity. A system that controls critical manufacturing parameters requires far more rigorous validation than one that generates internal reports. This concept prevents both under-validation (regulatory risk) and over-validation (wasted resource).

Example: A temperature monitoring system in a GMP warehouse (Cat 3, high criticality) receives full IQ/OQ/PQ with alarm testing and 21 CFR Part 11 controls. A document template management tool (Cat 2, low criticality) receives a simple fitness-for-use check and supplier documentation review.
Concept 02 — Life Cycle Thinking

Validation as a Continuous State

Validation is not an event — it is an ongoing state that must be actively maintained. From the moment a system is implemented to the day it is retired, it must remain in a validated state. Every change, update, or environmental modification must be assessed, controlled, and documented to maintain this state.

Example: When a cloud-hosted QMS receives an automatic update from the vendor, the customer organization must assess the update, test impacted workflows, update training materials if needed, and close the change request with QA approval before the update is considered validated.
Concept 03 — Supplier Leverage

Trust but Verify

GAMP 5 explicitly encourages organizations to leverage supplier testing evidence rather than duplicating it. If a supplier can demonstrate they have a mature quality management system, follow a defined SDLC, and provide comprehensive test documentation, the user organization can accept this as evidence — provided it is assessed and accepted formally.

Example: A LIMS vendor provides ISO 9001 certification, release notes, and factory acceptance test reports for their software. After a supplier audit confirms their QMS maturity, the customer accepts the vendor's IQ-equivalent documentation and focuses their own testing on configured laboratory workflows.
Concept 04 — Data Integrity (ALCOA+)

Trustworthy Data at the Core

Every GxP computerized system must ensure that data is: Attributable (who created it?), Legible (can it be read?), Contemporaneous (when was it recorded?), Original (is it the first capture?), Accurate (is it correct?) — plus Complete, Consistent, Enduring, and Available (the '+' additions).

Example: A chromatography data system must record the actual analyst login (Attributable), timestamp data at acquisition (Contemporaneous), prevent editing of raw data (Original), and provide an audit trail of all changes with who made them and why (Complete + Accurate).
Concept 05 — Science & Quality Risk Management

Evidence-Based Decision Making

All validation decisions in GAMP 5 should be grounded in scientific understanding and quality risk management principles (aligned with ICH Q9). This means using structured risk tools (FMEA, Fishbone, hazard analysis), making decisions based on evidence rather than habit, and documenting the scientific rationale for every validation choice.

Example: Using FMEA, a validation team identifies that the batch release approval workflow in an ERP system has a high failure impact score. This drives a decision to test that workflow with five different user scenarios, including negative testing and permission-level boundary checks — proportionate to the evidence-based risk score.
12 — Implementation Roadmap

Stages of GAMP 5

GAMP 5 implementation follows defined stages that apply to every validated system project. These stages translate the life cycle concept into practical, executable project phases — each producing specific deliverables and regulatory evidence.

1
🔍

Concept & Feasibility

Identify the business need, assess initial GxP impact, select technology approach, and confirm regulatory requirements that will apply. Go/no-go decision point.

2
📋

Project Planning & VP

Assign the validation team, produce the Validation Plan, define scope and deliverables, establish a project timeline, and obtain QA approval before proceeding.

3
📌

Requirements & Risk

Produce the URS documenting all functional and non-functional requirements. Conduct formal risk assessment to prioritize critical functions and determine testing scope.

4
🤝

Supplier Assessment

Evaluate the software vendor's quality system. Accept or reject supplier evidence. Establish a quality agreement. Define what IQ/OQ credit can be given to supplier documentation.

5
🏗️

Design & Configuration

Produce Functional and Design Specifications (where required by category). Configure the system against approved specifications. Document all configuration settings with version control.

6
🧪

Installation Qualification (IQ)

Verify that the system is correctly installed in the target environment. Confirm hardware, software versions, network, and security settings match specifications. Execute and sign IQ report.

7
⚙️

Operational Qualification (OQ)

Test that the system operates according to specifications. Test all critical functions, boundary conditions, error handling, audit trails, access controls, and data integrity features.

8
🔬

Performance Qualification (PQ)

Test the system under real business conditions — full process simulations, volume tests, concurrent user testing, and end-to-end workflow execution by actual users or their proxies.

9
📊

Validation Summary Report

Compile all test results, deviation resolutions, and outstanding actions into a Validation Summary Report (VSR). QA formally reviews and approves. This document authorizes GxP use.

10
🚀

Release & Go-Live

Train all users, migrate data (with data migration validation if applicable), execute cutover plan, and formally release the system to production. Archive all validation documentation.

11
🔧

Operational Maintenance

Operate the system under change control. Conduct periodic reviews (typically annual). Manage incidents, security, backups, and access rights. Every change assessed for validation impact.

12
🗃️

Retirement & Archival

When the system is decommissioned, produce a Retirement Plan. Migrate or archive all GxP data per regulatory retention periods. Ensure continued readability of archived records.

A system that is properly staged through all twelve GAMP 5 milestones leaves behind an irrefutable evidence package — the validation kit — that answers every inspector's question before it is asked: What did you build? What did you test? What went wrong? How did you fix it? And how do you know it still works today?
